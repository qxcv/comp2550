Localisation

Localisation is the task of determining your position within an environment given a series of sensor observations. Extremely important task for road vehicles.

Real world localisation is messy

  • GPS is unreliable
  • Dead reckoning drifts
  • More complex methods can be impractical

Improving the situation with maps

  1. Cars are on the road most of the time
  2. Detailed road maps are easy to find

How can we use this information to improve localisation?

Past work

Two broad families of “map matching” algorithms: heuristic and probabilistic

Heuristic methods use hand-coded rules to rule out unlikely vehicle positions

Probabilistic methods represent vehicle's position as a probability distribution over locations

Project goals

  1. Produce a map-based localisation algorithm which is robust to GPS dropouts
  2. Secondary goal: investigate performance of particle filters with “consumer grade” equipment and map data

Particle filters

Intuitively: represent vehicle's position using weighted “particles” distributed throughout the environment

  1. When vehicle moves, particles move
  2. Update particle weights to reflect sensor data likelihood
  3. Resample periodically to weed out low-weight particles

Example: Monte Carlo Localization: Efficient Position Estimation for Mobile Robots (Fox et al., 1999)

RHINO (pictured) was fitted with:

  • Sonar sensors
  • Laser range finders
  • Odometer

Why do particle filters work?

  • $z_t$ denotes the state of the vehicle (position, velocity, acceleration, etc.) at time $t$
  • $x_t$ denotes the observations made in state $z_t$ (gyroscope data, GPS readings, etc.)

\[ p(z_t \mid x_{1:t}) = \int\! p(z_t\mid z_{t-1}) p(z_{t-1}\mid x_{1:t-1}) \,dz_{t-1} \]
